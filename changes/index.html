Changes

This blog post is about change management.

In business, change is a constant element in daily operations. Successful business change strategies based on the current conditions of their environment. To track the effectiveness and impacts of changes made, it's critical that the changes be logged and monitored. Frequent changes in technology and the configuration of business infrastructure need to be recored and observed to validate results and ensure there are no unintended consequences. Change Management is a structured and careful approach to implement changes and to ensure the succesful completion of the change process.

Change management is a blanket term that encompasses the planning, approval, implementation, monitoring, and support of changes in business process or policies. One example of a change model is the Lean Change Management model. Some notable features of the Lean Change Management model is that change agents focus on creating shared purpose over creating false urgency, focus on enabling meaningul dialogue over broadcasting change communications, focus on experimenting over executing tasks in the plan, focus on understanging the response to change of blaming the resistance of the people effected, and focus on co-creation of change over getting participant buy-in.

Change management can be tailored for small, simple changes or complex, major changes. One main goal of change management is to layout a specific design of how a change should be completed to provide a clear and concise plan that all participants can easily understand. The result is a smoother completion of planned changes, as well as providing guidance and structure for unplanned changes. Beyond implementation alone, the change process should also include user feedback to understand the real world impact of changes.

To begin applying change management to your business, you must first gain a thorough understanding of the critical business procedures. Documentating the workflow of how a process should be carried out allows you to create the scafolding that will define exactly each step of a change process. A clear explanation of the desired outcome from a change will improve the help narrow the focus of the process.

Change management should also seek out the involvement of everyone effected by a new change process. Identifying key change leaders that will champion the new change are critical to creating a culture of change, and they will be a driving force in the implemenation of new processes. Communication before, during, and after a change provides transparency to end users and increases the success rate of new changes. People, who can be resistent to change, are more likely to adopt a new business proceess if they understand the reasoning behind the decision. Milestones, which are specific goals on a timeline, help track the progress of a change throughout the entirity of the process.

As part of creating new processes, change managers should also identify and assign a change owner. Have a change owner creates a sense of accountabilty and ownership, which is part of the overall aim of the culture of change. Consideration should be taken for the human element of change, which requires that members of the organization are receptive to new changes, and practiced at modifying existing processes. People are emotional beings, so it may be necessary to provide sympathetic support beyond the logical explantaion of how a process can be improved. When properly applied, change management can get people excited about improving current procedures by implementing change.
