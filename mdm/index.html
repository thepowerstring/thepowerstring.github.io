MDM

This blog post is about mobile device management.

Mobile Device Management (or MDM for short) is the practice of using administrative policies to control all sorts of portable devices, including mobile phones, tablets, laptops, and more. In the enterprise, this can mean corporate owned devices (in the cases where the business owns and maintains the mobile devices) and Bring Your Own Device, also known as BYOD (where the end user owns the device, and the business manages the settings for applications). For BYOD, some devices support operating system partitions that allow separating the corporate and personal data.

Mobile device management can come in the form of geofencing, where a device is only allowed to connect while within certain GPS coodrinates, creating a real-world, global boundry of device operation. MDM is also used for the remote wiping of devices when they are reported missing or stolen. Mobile Device Management can also mean applying administrative policies that determine specific settings on the device, like whether a PIN is required for device access, or whether not system updates are installed automatically. Likewise, restrictions can be defined using a MDM product's management console to prevent an endpoint device from connecting unless it has the latest updates installed and virus scan run recently, for example.

One challenge to Mobile Device Management is managing different types of operating systems. Some settings are only supported on certain operating systems, but most MDM platforms support dynamic grouping of like device types for easily applying specific management policies to a group of predefined devices.

Microsoft's platform for Mobile Device Management is called Intune, and for Windows devices it uses the Open Mobile Alliance Uniform Resource Identifier (OMA-URI) configuration service provider (or CSP) to define policies and enforce management policies. The Open Mobile Alliance Device Management (OMA-DM) protocol uses the XML-based SyncML to transfer data between servers and clients. According to Microsoft's documentation, Configuration Service Providers are simlar to Group Policy client-side extensions in that they provide an interface to read, set, modify, or delete configuration settings for a given feature. The settings typically map to registry keys, files, or permissions. Some settings can be configured, but others are read-only. Many additional Mobile Device Management vendors offer MDM Platform as a Service for configuring mobile devices.

The Open Mobile Alliance (also known as OMA) is a non-profit standards organization dedicated to to the mobile telecommunications industry. The mission of the OMA is to provide interoperability of services on the global stage, and that means working with different types of devices in different parts of the world. This includes devices from various manufacturers, including Android and Apple iOS. Some other examples of other MDM platforms are VMware AirWatch, Cisco's Meraki MDM, and SimpleMDM.

MDM allows for the deployment and management of mobile devices at scale. As such, it is a critical component of modern infrastucture. Another advantage of Mobile Device Management is visibility into device usage and security posture, even without being network connected in the case of cloud service providers. MDM also helps reduce or eliminate configuration errors by providing consistency through standardizing the management processses for mobile devices.
