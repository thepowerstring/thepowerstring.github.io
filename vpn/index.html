VPN

This blog post is about virtual private networks.

VPN is an acronym that stands for Virtual Private Network, which is an encrypted tunnel that connects two devices over the Internet. VPNs are used to extend a private network across a public network. VPNs protect traffic by preventing bad actors from eavesdropping on the data, ensuring safe transmission of sensitive information. The devices that are connected using a point to point tunnel established with a VPN can be two firewalls in different office branch locations, as in the case of a site to site VPN. Site to site VPN connections between offices allow for a secure, direct link using public infrastructure. VPNs that are used for workstation computers and mobile devices to connect to a remote network are referred to as remote access VPNs. The remote clients using a Remote Access VPN are known as endpoints, and security policies can be implemented to ensure they're in a certain state before being allowed to connect.

VPNs are usually installed on endpoints using client software that's native to the operating system (sometimes called network-based VPNs), or by running a 3rd party application (referred to as client VPNs). Virtual private networks can sometimes be turned on and off by the end user. Where the VPN connection isn't optional, the connection is referred to as being "Always On" or always connected. In the case of Always On VPNs, network traffic may not all be routed over the VPN tunnel. Split-tunnel VPNs are so-called becuase they route certain traffic destined for remote networks over the VPN, and traffic not destined for those subnets is directed to the standard network interface. Using a spliit tunnel reduces the demand placed on the remote appliance that handles VPN traffic by routing only the required data over the VPN. The opposite of a split-tunnel is a full tunnel VPN, which routes all traffic over the VPN before forwarding on to its final desitnation.

VPNs are not required to implement encryption to operate, but they are often synonamous with increased security. Multiple technologies can be used to secure VPNs, including IPsec (also known as Internet Protocol Security) and SSL / TLS (also respectively known as Secure Socket Layer and Transport Layer Security). DTLS (or Datagram Transport Layer Security) is utilized by the Cisco AnyConnect client to overcome the limitation of TLS tunneling over TCP. Microsoft supports the Point to Point tunneling protocol (as referred to as PPTP), along with other more moderna and secure offerings, like SSTP (also known as Secure Socket Tunneling Protocol) and OpenSSH. OpenSSH, based off of the SSH (Secure SHell) protocol, can be also used for VPN tunneling. LT2P, also known as Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol, is another protocol used to create Virtual Private Networks. LT2P does not provide encryption for the data being sent, and is often used in combination with IPsec to provide confidentiality.

VPNs can also be used to bypass georestrictions because the traffic shows as originating from the VPN tunnel's exit, instead of the endpoint's network connection. VPNs can coexist with multiple other network connections and traffic can be routed over different interfaces seamlessly. Some downsides to using a VPN are slower network speeds due to the increased distance traveled, as well increased netowrk complexity.
