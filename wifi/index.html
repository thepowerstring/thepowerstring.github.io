Wi-Fi Standards

This blog post is about wi-fi standards.

The IEEE (abbreviation for Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) 802.11 technical standards define different specifications for wireless communication, also known as Wi-Fi. The 802.11 specifications allow for standardization between different industres when manufacturing consumer and business products. Pre-defined standards prevent manufacturers from creating their own proprietary implementations, which increases interoperability of devices, as well as ensuring devices marketed using the "Wi-Fi" brand are able to communicate with each other.

In a typical wireless local area network, physcial cards use antennas to transmit radio waves to recipient antennas on an access point. Repeaters sometimes do the job of extending the range of a wireless radio by receiving the signal from one device and re-transmitting the signal. Many Wi-Fi antennas are omnidirectional, meaning they broadcast in all directions.

Wireless communication can use a range of frequencies across the radio spectrum, but some of the most common frequencies in use are the 2.4 and 5 Ghz bands. Within the each frequency, there is a predefined set of channels available. Wi-fi communication is subject to electromagnetic interference. Nearby wiresless networks using the same frequency, or devices that emit radio waves in the frequency being used for modultion, can cause interference that slows or prevents normal operation. Physical interference with radio waves can occur when attempting to transmit radio signals through certain materials. Reflection of wireless signals can occur when metal or conductive materials are in the signal's transmission path. Concrete with steel reinforcement typically interferes with wireless transmission more than glass.

Wireless communication is limited to half-duplex, meaning the sender and receiver cannot tranmit simulataneously. To prevent traffic collisions during transmission, 802.11 uses the 'carrier-sense multiple access with collision avoidance' protocol, abbreviated CSMA/CA. CSMA/CA operates in the Data Link Layer (also known as Layer 2) of the OSI model. To avoid collisions during transmission, CSMA/CA waits to transmit network traffic until the channel is found to be in an idle state. Because of weakness in the CSMA/CA protocol, efforts are being made to improve its operation by expanding on the existing functionality.

Wi-Fi 6 is the latest iteration of the 802.11 family of specifications. The specific standard for Wi-Fi 6 is Wi-Fi-802.11ax, and it operates on frequencies between 1 and 6 Ghz. The predecessor to Wi-Fi 6 is Wi-Fi 5, which is defined in the Wi-Fi-802.11ac sepecification, and it uses the 5 Ghz band. Wi-Fi 5 also introduced standardized beamforming technology, which transmits signals to a specific user instead of broadcasting the signal like previous standards. 802.11n (also known as Wi-Fi 4) uses the 2.4 Ghz range, which is considered to be more prone to interence due to congestion in this frequency range.

Newer wireless standards provide better security by using stronger encryption algorithms. Wi-Fi Protected Access, or WPA, and the subsequent versions (WPA2, WPA3) offer improved security over WEP, also known as Wired Equivalent Privacy. WEP utilied the now deprecated RC4 stream cipher for confidentiality, and the CRC-32 checksum for integrity. WEP's successor, WPA used TKIP (abbreviation for Temporal Key Integrity Portal) to dynamically generate a new key for each packet.

Modern wireless communication utilizes MIMO, or multiple input and multiple output to increase the available bandwidth of a wireless radio by using multiple antennas. Other wireless technologies that are not realted to Wi-Fi are Bluetooth / BLE, ZigBee, WiMax, and LTE / Cellular Networks.
